Sicily · Italy

Across Sicily: old streets, special pasta, and Marsala

A journey remembered through changing cities, local food, warm Mediterranean light, and the remarkable depth of Marsala wine.

Sicily felt different from place to place. Moving through its cities and smaller towns, I kept noticing how quickly the atmosphere changed—from busy streets and layered architecture to quieter corners shaped by the sea. Yet the warm stone, strong light, and generous relationship with food connected the journey.

Remembering Sicily through food

Food became one of the clearest ways to understand the island. I tried local dishes along the route, but one of the meals I remember most was a pasta particular to the area. Its shape and texture held the sauce beautifully, making a seemingly simple plate feel distinctive and deeply connected to the place. It was the kind of meal that would be difficult to recreate elsewhere—not only because of the ingredients, but because of the surrounding streets, voices, and pace of the day.

What stayed with me was the confidence of Sicilian cooking. The flavors did not need to be complicated to be memorable: good pasta, local ingredients, and combinations refined over generations were enough.

Marsala: more than a name on a bottle

Another highlight was visiting a Marsala winery and production cellar. Before the visit, I knew Marsala mainly as the famous wine associated with western Sicily. Seeing where it is produced made it feel much more tangible—the barrels, the aging process, the changing color of the wine, and the patience required before a bottle is ready.

I was especially surprised by the range of aged Marsala. Some wines had spent many years developing in the cellar, becoming deeper, more complex, and more layered over time. Tasting the older expressions was amazing: each carried a sense of the wood, the climate, and the long period of aging. The visit changed my understanding of Marsala from a single style of wine into a whole tradition.

What I brought home

The strongest memories are small ones: a plate of special pasta after a long walk, evening light settling over old buildings, and the quiet atmosphere of a cellar where the wine had been aging for years. Sicily is a place I remember through taste, color, texture, and time—and through the photographs that will accompany this diary.
